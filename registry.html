How Sex Offender Registries Work

Registries were established under federal legislation — most importantly the Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act of 2006 (SORNA) — which created national minimum standards while leaving each state to run its own system. That's why the search experience varies so much: some states let you search by name only, others by address radius, others show interactive maps. All of them are authoritative for offenders registered in that state, and all of them roll up into the NSOPW national search.

The three federal tiers

Under SORNA, registered offenses fall into three tiers based on the underlying crime, and the tier determines how often the person must re-register and how long they remain on the registry:

  • Tier I — the least serious registrable offenses; typically 15 years of registration with annual verification
  • Tier II — more serious offenses; 25 years, verification every six months
  • Tier III — the most serious offenses, including offenses against children; lifetime registration with quarterly verification

Not every state uses the federal tier system directly — some use their own classification (Levels 1/2/3, or "Sexually Violent Predator" designations) — but every registered record includes the underlying conviction, so you're never guessing at severity when you read a listing.

What registries do — and don't — tell you

A public registry entry generally shows the person's name, photograph, physical description, current registered address, the offense of conviction, and often the date of last verification. What it does not tell you is whether that person has committed any new offenses since — only the underlying conviction that put them on the registry in the first place. Registries also don't include people whose offenses fall outside registration requirements, unregistered individuals in violation, or people accused but not convicted.

Use registry data responsibly. These lookups exist to help families make informed safety decisions. Harassment, vigilantism, or discrimination based on registry status is illegal in every state and undermines the system's public-safety purpose. If you have a specific concern, contact local law enforcement.

Practical searches families actually run

  • Before moving: check the destination address against the state registry's map view. Most state sites let you draw a radius or view a neighborhood-level map.
  • Before hiring a babysitter, tutor, or caregiver: a name search on NSOPW covers every state at once.
  • When your kid starts spending time at a friend's house: the map view over your area gives context without requiring a specific name.
  • Buying a house: the registry check pairs naturally with the safety and hazard research on our other tools — pattern captured in our neighborhood research guide.

If information looks wrong

Every state registry publishes a contact for reporting errors or apparent unregistered offenders. Because we don't reproduce registry data on this site, corrections always need to go to the source — the state's own registry office is the only entity that can update a record.
