The 10 Safest Cities in the U.S. — 2026 Ranking

Updated July 2026 · Written by the Safety & Crime team

A ranking of "the safest cities in America" only means something if you know how it was built. Most viral lists sort by a single crime index and stop there — which produces skewed results (very small towns dominate simply because a handful of incidents move a rate less than in bigger cities) and ignores the tradeoffs that make a "safe" city actually a good place to live. This ranking does it differently: real methodology upfront, honest tradeoffs for every pick, and links to live data so you can verify anything you see.

Our methodology, in one paragraph: we started with a pool of U.S. cities with populations of at least 50,000 — big enough that crime rates aren't statistical noise. We scored each on regional violent and property crime estimates (see our methodology page for how those are built), real Census median household income and educational attainment, unemployment, and natural hazard exposure from the tools on this site (flood, earthquake, wildfire). We removed cities whose "safety" number rested on being too small to measure reliably. The result: cities that are quantitatively safer and big enough that the number means something.

What no ranking can capture: visiting is not optional. Safety statistics can't tell you whether the streets feel comfortable at 10pm, whether the neighborhood four blocks over is different from the one on the cover photo, or whether the city fits your life. Use this list as a shortlist, not a decision.

Why it's here

Naperville routinely tops "best places to live" rankings and consistently reports among the lowest violent crime rates for a city its size in the country. It combines nationally-ranked public schools (Naperville District 203, Indian Prairie District 204), a strong professional job base, and a walkable, well-planned downtown along the Riverwalk. Median household income is well above national and regional averages.

Real tradeoffs

Housing costs and property taxes are high — Illinois property taxes are among the nation's steepest, and Naperville's home values compound the bill. Winters are genuine Midwest cold. Commuting into central Chicago takes ~45 minutes by Metra rail; driving in bad weather is longer.

Why it's here

Irvine is remarkable: a large, master-planned city that has ranked among the safest in America for large cities for decades running. UC Irvine anchors a well-educated population, corporate campuses provide a solid tax base, and the Irvine Company's original master plan produced an unusually coherent, walkable city fabric. Serious violent crime is genuinely rare here.

Real tradeoffs

Southern California cost of living, especially housing — Irvine home prices routinely top $1.2M median. Seismic risk from nearby faults is real (see earthquake data). Traffic between Orange and Los Angeles counties can be brutal. Some critics find the master-planned aesthetic sterile compared to older SoCal cities.

Why it's here

Cary sits inside one of the country's strongest job markets (Research Triangle Park is 15 minutes away), and the town has managed rapid growth with better-than-typical infrastructure investment. Consistently low crime, Wake County Public Schools access, and a milder cost of living than California or Northeast equivalents make it a common landing spot for tech relocations.

Real tradeoffs

Growth has outpaced traffic capacity in parts of the metro; commutes have gotten worse. The area is not hurricane-immune — inland North Carolina still sees significant tropical storm impact. Summer humidity is intense.

Why it's here

One of the fastest-growing large cities in America and one of the safest, Frisco has attracted corporate headquarters (including major sports and tech), which brought high-income households and heavy infrastructure investment. Frisco ISD is a national reputation. Property tax offsets Texas's no-income-tax advantage more than newcomers expect.

Real tradeoffs

Growth pressure on schools and roads is real. North Texas storm season brings hail, tornadoes, and severe weather — check local weather in spring and summer. Property tax rates approach 2.5% of home value effective annually.

Why it's here

Gilbert transformed from a small farming town into one of the safest large cities in America over about two decades. Family-friendly infrastructure, well-planned neighborhoods, and a growing job base make it a common relocation choice. Cost of living remains lower than Phoenix-area alternatives with similar quality.

Why it's here

The urban outlier on this list — Cambridge is a dense, walkable, transit-connected city and still posts notably low violent crime for its size and density. Harvard and MIT anchor the population and economy, public schools are well-funded, and the transit access alone is a quality-of-life factor most safe cities can't match.

Real tradeoffs

Housing is genuinely expensive — some of the highest per-square-foot prices in the country. Winters are cold and long. Property crime (mostly bike theft and package theft) runs higher than the outer-suburb picks on this list — very low violent crime doesn't mean nothing happens.

Cities That Nearly Made This List

Any top-10 leaves off cities that plausibly belong. Honorable mentions worth searching if you're relocating: Plano, TX (Dallas metro, safety profile similar to Frisco with more established feel); Chandler, AZ (Phoenix metro, similar profile to Gilbert with slightly older housing stock); Franklin, TN (Nashville metro, growing rapidly with strong safety metrics); Bellevue, WA (Seattle metro, high-income tech hub with lower crime than Seattle proper); and Fishers, IN (Indianapolis metro, consistent low-crime suburb).

How to Use This Ranking

  1. Treat the top 10 as candidates worth researching, not a shopping list
  2. Use the "Safety data →" link on each city to see actual ZIP-level context
  3. Layer in the non-crime factors — flood risk, wildfires, weather patterns, cost of living
  4. Run the comfortable income calculation for any ZIPs you're serious about
  5. Follow the full neighborhood research process before committing to anything
  6. Visit. At multiple times of day. On foot. With two neighbor conversations. No online guide replaces that.
