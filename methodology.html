Our Data & Methodology

Every tool on Safety & Crime is built on publicly available data, and we believe you should know exactly where each number comes from — including which figures are official government statistics and which are our own estimates. This page explains all of it, tool by tool.

Official Source · U.S. Census Bureau

Population & Home Values

Population and median home value figures on our ZIP Code Search come directly from the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey (ACS) 5-Year estimates, queried live for each ZIP Code Tabulation Area (ZCTA). When a figure comes from the Census, the card is labeled "Census" and the expanded detail panel lists the source. For a small number of ZIP codes the Census has no ZCTA record — typically P.O. Box-only ZIPs or very new ones — and in those cases we fall back to a regional estimate, clearly labeled "Est."

The ACS 5-Year survey is the most reliable public source for small-area demographics in the United States. Because it aggregates five years of survey responses, figures update annually but represent a rolling average rather than a single point in time.

Modeled Estimate · Not Official Statistics

Safety Scores & Crime Rates

Our safety scores and crime rate figures are statistical estimates, not official police data. The United States has no unified, publicly available database of crime statistics at the ZIP code level — police agencies report at the department or jurisdiction level, coverage is voluntary and incomplete, and ZIP codes don't align with police district boundaries. Any website showing "crime by ZIP code" is modeling, whether it says so or not. We say so.

How the estimates work

Our model assigns each ZIP code a baseline derived from the crime profile of its broader region (using the ZIP's prefix, which groups geographically related areas), adjusted for population density patterns and urban/suburban/rural classification. Violent and property crime figures are expressed per 100,000 residents to make areas comparable. The Safety Score (0–100) and letter grade summarize these estimates relative to national patterns — a score of 64 means the model places that ZIP as safer than roughly 64% of U.S. ZIP codes.

What the estimates are good for — and not

These estimates are useful for getting a general sense of how an area compares to national patterns and for side-by-side comparisons of regions. They are not suitable for insurance, legal, lending, or security decisions, and they cannot capture block-by-block variation within a ZIP code. For official statistics, we recommend the FBI's Crime Data Explorer and your local police department's published reports, both linked from our About page.

Official Source · National Weather Service

Weather Forecasts

Every forecast on our Weather tool comes live from the National Weather Service API — the same forecasts produced by NWS meteorologists at 120+ regional offices. Supplementary metrics in the detail view (humidity, feels-like temperature, wind gusts, pressure, visibility, UV index, sunrise/sunset) come from Open-Meteo, an open-data weather service, and air quality readings use the EPA's US AQI scale via Open-Meteo's air quality feed. Radar imagery is provided by RainViewer, showing real weather radar from the past two hours plus a short-term projection. Moon phases are computed astronomically and don't rely on any external source.

Official Source · U.S. Geological Survey

Earthquake Data

Our Earthquake tool queries the USGS earthquake catalog live for every search: recorded quakes within roughly 100 miles of the searched location over the past 30 days, plus significant (M4+) historical events over the past 50 years. Magnitudes, depths, times, and locations are exactly as recorded by the USGS seismic network. The "Seismic Risk" label (Low / Moderate / Elevated) is our simple summary based on the count and size of historical quakes in the area — it's a readability aid, not an official USGS hazard rating. For engineering-grade hazard data, see the USGS National Seismic Hazard Model.

Official Sources · FEMA, NWS & USGS

Flood Data

The Flood tool combines three official sources. Flood zone designations come from FEMA's National Flood Hazard Layer — the same maps used for federal flood insurance requirements — queried at the searched ZIP code's center point. Active flood warnings, watches, and advisories come live from the National Weather Service alert system. River and stream levels come from USGS Water Services' network of real-time gauges.

One important caveat: a ZIP code covers a wide area, and flood zones can change from one street to the next. Our lookup checks the ZIP's central point, so it's a starting reference — for a property-specific determination (the kind lenders require), use FEMA's Flood Map Service Center with the exact address.

Corrections & Updates

Live data (weather, earthquakes, flood alerts, river gauges) refreshes on every search. Census figures update when the Census Bureau publishes new ACS releases, typically annually. Our regional crime estimates are reviewed and recalibrated periodically. If you believe any figure on this site is wrong or misleading, we genuinely want to know — reach out via the contact information on our About page and we'll investigate.
