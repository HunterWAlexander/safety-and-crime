Safest Neighborhoods in Florida 2026: A Data-Backed Guide

Updated July 2026 · Written by the Safety & Crime team

Florida's population growth over the past decade has been enormous, and choosing a safe neighborhood in a state that's still rapidly changing requires more than a crime score. Hurricane exposure, flood zones (Florida has more properties in FEMA flood zones than any other state), and the ongoing insurance crisis are as central to the decision as which streets have the lowest crime. This guide covers the safest neighborhoods and suburbs across Florida's major metros, with real tradeoffs for each.

How this guide is built: we combine regional safety score estimates (methodology here), U.S. Census population and income data, and Florida-specific hazard data from our flood, weather, and tsunami tools. Every neighborhood links to live data.
The Florida-specific caveat: "safest" here means low crime and strong community context. Every coastal Florida neighborhood carries hurricane exposure, and homeowners insurance costs have risen dramatically statewide. Check specific address-level flood zone and get an actual insurance quote before committing to any Florida location.

Considerations Specific to Florida

Insurance is the story of Florida real estate right now

Homeowners insurance premiums in Florida have risen dramatically in recent years, and several major carriers have exited the state entirely. Citizens Property Insurance (the state-run insurer of last resort) has grown to cover a significant share of policies. Before committing to any Florida address, get an actual insurance quote from active carriers — not just an estimate — because prices in similar neighborhoods can differ by thousands of dollars depending on wind mitigation, roof age, and flood zone.

Flood insurance is separate and often required

Standard homeowners insurance in Florida does not cover flood. If your address is in a FEMA flood zone and you have a mortgage, flood insurance through the National Flood Insurance Program is typically required. Even properties technically outside high-risk zones have been damaged by inland flooding from tropical systems — Hurricane Ian's inland flooding in 2022 hit areas that had been considered low-risk. Use the flood tool at the specific ZIP, and consider the general policy anyway.

Storm surge vs. wind vs. inland flooding are three different problems

Coastal properties face storm surge (rising ocean water pushed inland). All Florida properties face hurricane wind exposure. Inland areas face rain-driven flooding from stalled systems. Any specific address's real risk profile is a combination of all three, and different construction standards address different threats. The question isn't "is this address safe from hurricanes?" — it's "which of the three do I actually need to defend against, and how?"
