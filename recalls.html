How Safety Recalls Work

A recall happens when a manufacturer or a federal agency determines that a product creates an unreasonable safety risk or fails to meet minimum safety standards. Vehicle recalls are overseen by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA); most other consumer products fall under the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC). Both agencies publish every recall publicly — the data on this page comes live from their official databases.

Vehicle Recalls: What Owners Should Know

Roughly one in five vehicles on U.S. roads has an open, unrepaired recall. Manufacturers must fix recalled defects free of charge at franchised dealerships — there is never a fee for recall repair, regardless of the vehicle's age or how many owners it's had. A year/make/model search like the one above shows every campaign affecting that model; for certainty about your specific car (some recalls only cover certain production runs), run your 17-character VIN through NHTSA's official VIN tool. If you're buying a used car, checking recalls before purchase takes two minutes and can reveal unrepaired safety defects the seller may not even know about.

Product Recalls: Why They're Easy to Miss

Unlike vehicles, most consumer products aren't registered to an owner — so when a crib, space heater, or battery pack is recalled, the manufacturer usually has no way to contact buyers directly. Recalls of children's products deserve particular attention: nursery products, toys, and furniture account for a large share of CPSC actions, often for tip-over, choking, or entrapment hazards. Checking the feed above periodically — or before buying secondhand items — is one of the simplest safety habits a household can build. Remedies typically include free repair kits, replacement, or full refunds.
