Safest Neighborhoods in Texas 2026: A Data-Backed Guide

Updated July 2026 · Written by the Safety & Crime team, based in Houston

Texas is enormous. Ranking "the safest neighborhoods in Texas" as a single list treats a state larger than Germany like a small town, and the results usually come out useless — a dozen tiny suburbs of Austin nobody's heard of, or a raw score list with no explanation. This guide takes a different approach: we group Texas by major metro, spotlight the neighborhoods that consistently score well across multiple safety and quality-of-life dimensions, and — critically — explain why each one earns its spot and what tradeoffs come with it.

How this guide is built: we combine our regional safety score estimates (methodology here), U.S. Census population and income data, and public data on flood zones, weather, and local infrastructure. Every neighborhood links to a live data lookup so you can verify and dig deeper.

Houston Area

The Houston metro's safest neighborhoods cluster in the western and northwestern suburbs — areas with strong school districts, active homeowners associations, and (importantly for the Gulf Coast) less flood exposure than inner-loop neighborhoods that straddle bayous.

The Woodlands
ZIP 77380 / 77381 / 77382 · ~30 miles north of Houston
Low crimeMaster-plannedFamily favorite
A master-planned community that consistently ranks among the safest places to live in Texas. Low crime rates, top-tier public schools (Conroe ISD), extensive greenbelt and trail systems, and a walkable Town Center. Median home values are well above the Houston metro average — the safety comes at a cost premium. Flood risk is relatively low compared to central Houston, though not zero; several 2015 and 2016 events reached parts of the community.

Sugar Land
ZIP 77479 / 77478 · Southwest suburbs
Low crimeDiverseStrong schools
Sugar Land routinely appears on national "best places to live" lists thanks to Fort Bend ISD's academic reputation, one of the most ethnically diverse populations in the country, and consistently low crime estimates. Development has been more measured than surrounding areas, which helped it maintain quality infrastructure. Worth checking specific ZIPs for flood zone — parts of Fort Bend saw significant Harvey impact in 2017.

Katy (Cinco Ranch area)
ZIP 77494 · West Houston suburbs
Family-orientedGrowing
Cinco Ranch and the greater Katy area draw families for Katy ISD, well-maintained subdivisions, and a lower cost of entry than The Woodlands or Sugar Land. Rapid growth has created traffic and school-capacity challenges. Flood plain awareness is essential here — some 77494 sub-neighborhoods flooded during Harvey despite being marketed as "not in a flood zone."

Bellaire
ZIP 77401 · Inner-loop suburb
Central locationEstablished
A small, independent city fully surrounded by Houston, offering low crime, a strong school district (Bellaire is part of HISD but the local schools consistently perform), and a location much closer to the Medical Center and downtown than the outer suburbs. Home prices reflect all of that. Elevation is a real consideration; some Bellaire streets flood in heavy rainfall.

Dallas–Fort Worth

DFW's safest neighborhoods span both cities' northern suburbs, where the combination of newer construction, well-funded school districts, and lower crime density create some of Texas's most sought-after zip codes.

Frisco
ZIP 75033 / 75034 / 75035 · North of Plano
Low crimeJob growthNew construction
Frisco has been one of the fastest-growing large cities in America for years, and the growth has come with strong safety statistics and top-ranked schools (Frisco ISD). Corporate relocations — including major sports and tech companies — brought high-income households and infrastructure investment. Traffic and school overcrowding are the current tensions of that growth.

Small in population, extraordinary in reputation — Southlake's Carroll ISD has produced consistent state-championship level programs both academically and athletically, and crime rates are near the lowest in the metro. Entry prices are among the highest in Texas outside of central Austin.

Austin Area

Central Austin has real crime and cost pressures, so this section leans toward the metro's outer ring — where you get lower crime estimates, more space, and better prices, at the cost of longer commutes and less of the city character people move to Austin for.

Considerations Specific to Texas

Hurricanes and flooding aren't just a coastal concern

Any Texas neighborhood you're considering should get a flood risk check. Hurricane Harvey demonstrated that flooding can devastate areas well inland, and Texas's flat terrain plus intense rainfall patterns make flash flooding a factor in many "safe on paper" neighborhoods. Coastal metros — Houston, Corpus Christi, the Rio Grande Valley — face additional hurricane wind exposure.

Property tax matters as much as safety

Texas has no state income tax, but property tax rates are among the highest in the nation. Some of the neighborhoods above have effective property tax rates approaching 3% of home value annually — real money that belongs in your comparison alongside safety statistics.

School district reputation moves with the years

Many of the neighborhoods above are built on their school district reputations. Districts change — bond failures, boundary redraws, and staff turnover matter — so recent local data beats stale rankings when picking a specific address.
