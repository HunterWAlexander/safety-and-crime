Buying a Used Car? Check for Open Recalls First

Published July 2026 · 7 min read · By the Safety & Crime team

Here's a statistic that should change how you shop for used cars: roughly one in five vehicles on American roads has an open, unrepaired safety recall. Not a squeaky belt or a worn tire — a defect serious enough that the manufacturer or federal regulators concluded it poses an unreasonable safety risk. Airbags that can send shrapnel. Fuel pumps that quit on the highway. Brake components that corrode. Software that lets the engine stall in an intersection.

And here's the part that makes checking a no-brainer: every one of those defects gets fixed for free. Federal law requires manufacturers to repair recalled defects at no charge at any franchised dealer, no matter how old the car is or how many owners it's had. The check takes two minutes. The repair costs nothing. The only losing move is not looking.

Why used cars are the recall blind spot

When a recall is issued, the manufacturer mails notices to registered owners. That system works reasonably well for the first owner — and starts leaking immediately after. Cars change hands; owners move; notices go to old addresses; private-sale buyers never enter the manufacturer's records at all. Each resale makes it less likely anyone ever sees the letter.

There's also a gap many buyers don't know about: used-car dealers are generally not required by federal law to fix open recalls before selling you the car. (New-car dealers are barred from selling new vehicles with open recalls — used inventory plays by different rules.) A used car can sit on a lot, sail through the dealer's "inspection," and roll off with a defective airbag inflator that a franchised dealer would have replaced for free. The system quietly assumes you'll check. So check.

How to check any car in two minutes

  1. Start with year, make, and model. Our free Recall Lookup tool searches the official NHTSA database and shows every recall campaign affecting that model — the component, the defect, the danger, and the fix. This is perfect for the shopping phase, when you're comparing candidates and don't have VINs yet.
  2. Before money changes hands, run the VIN. Some recalls only affect certain production runs, and the seller's car may already have been repaired. The 17-character VIN (visible through the windshield on the driver's side, and on the door-jamb sticker) gets you a definitive answer for that exact vehicle at NHTSA's official VIN tool (nhtsa.gov/recalls). "Zero open recalls" there means this specific car, today.
  3. If a recall is open, decide before you buy. Ask the seller to have it repaired first (it costs them nothing but a dealer visit), or price the hassle into your offer and book the repair yourself immediately after purchase. For serious defects — anything involving airbags, brakes, steering, or fire risk — insist on the repair first or walk away. Some recalled vehicles carry official "do not drive" or "park outside" warnings.
Two-minute habit for current owners: this isn't just a buying tip. Check your own car twice a year — recalls are issued continuously, often years after a model ships. Set a reminder for when the clocks change, like smoke-detector batteries.

What to know about the repair

  • It's free at franchised dealers of that brand — any of them, not just where the car was bought. A used Honda from a stranger on Craigslist gets free recall repairs at any Honda dealer.
  • No proof of purchase needed. The recall follows the VIN, not the owner.
  • Parts can take time. Large recalls sometimes outpace parts supply; you may be scheduled weeks out. Get in the queue early — and ask the dealer whether interim precautions apply.
  • "Recall" is not "warranty." Warranties expire; recall obligations don't. A 15-year-old car with an open recall still gets the free fix.

Questions worth asking any used-car seller

  • "Are there any open recalls on this VIN?" — then verify yourself; honest sellers won't mind
  • "Do you have records showing recall work was completed?" — dealer service records list campaign numbers
  • "Would you have the open recall fixed before we close the deal?" — free for them, revealing if they refuse
Red flag: a seller who shrugs off a serious open recall — especially airbag or fire-risk campaigns — is telling you how they treated the rest of the car's maintenance.

Beyond the car: the products in your garage

Vehicles at least have a notification system. Most consumer products — the space heater, the kids' car seat, the battery charger sitting in your trunk — have no registration at all, so when they're recalled, nobody calls. Our Recalls page also shows the latest CPSC consumer product recalls, updated continuously; it's worth a scan whenever you buy secondhand baby gear or electronics. And if you're researching a move along with a vehicle purchase, our ZIP Code Search, Flood, and Weather tools cover the where while the recall tools cover the what.

The bottom line

  • One in five cars carries an open recall; used cars are the most likely to slip through
  • Used-car dealers generally aren't required to fix recalls before selling — the check is on you
  • Model-level search while shopping, VIN-level check before buying
  • Repairs are free, forever, at any franchised dealer of the brand
  • Serious defect + unwilling seller = walk away

Two minutes of checking against a defect someone else already documented, on a repair someone else already has to pay for — there may be no better return on effort in all of car buying.
